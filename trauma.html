Assessment

Trauma

Equipment:

  • c-collar
  • tourniquets
  • trauma dressings, petroleum dressings

Fab Five

  • BSI
  • scene safety
  • nature of illness
  • # of patients
  • additional resources

AVPU

  • Holding c-spine
  • Sir are you ok, are you ok?
  • Pinching the ear and trap
  • Patient is unresponsive

Airway

  • Opening the airway with a jaw thrust and assessing for patency
  • Do I have patency?
    • Yes » Sizing & inserting an OPA, measuring from corner of the mouth to bottom of the ear Does the patient accept?
    • No »  My partner will suction for 15 seconds, do I have patency?

Breathing

  • Looking, listening and feeling for rate rhythm and quality of breathing for 5-10 seconds
  • Visualizing the chest for equal chest rise and any obvious injuries
    • Obvious chest injury »
      • Partner grab c-spine so I can treat the chest injury
      • Open Wound: petroleum, occlusive dressing taped on 3 sides
      • Penetration: stabilize penetration in place
      • Flail segment: stabilize flail segment with trauma dressing
    • No obvious chest injury »
      • Continue
  • My partner will administer oxygen at 15 liters/minute via non-rebreather
  • and take over c-spine so I can assess circulation

Circulation

  • Checking carotid and radial pulses for rate rhythm and quality for 5-10 seconds
  • Checking cap refill
  • Visualizing the skin for color, temperature, condition
  • Exposing the chest, visualizing the entire body & checking voids for any major bleeding Look at gloves after each void check
  • Do I find anything?
    • Bleeding from femoral artery: applying direct pressure for 1 minute; bleeding doesn't stop - apply tourniquet

    Rapid Assessment

    • Initial assessment is complete, we'll stay on scene for a rapid assessment
    • and look for DCAPBLSTIC - deformities, contusions, abrasions, penetrations, burns, lacerations, swelling, tenderness, instability, crepitus

    Head

    • Palpating the bones of the skull: frontal, occipital, temporal, parietal
    • Checking the pupils for equality and reactivity
    • Checking the nose and ears for any blood or CSF
    • Do I find anything?
      • YES » Covering that with a loose dressing
    • Palpating the orbits, zygomas, maxilla and mandible
    • Reassessing a patent airway and adequate oxygen delivery

    Neck

    • Assessing the anterior neck for trach shift, or JVD
    • Palpating the posterior neck for any stepoff
    • Sizing and applying a collar, measuring fingers from the jaw line to the nape of the neck

    Chest/Sternum

    • Palpating the scapulas and clavicles
    • Palpating the thorax looking for any subcue air
    • Assessing stability of the ribcage
    • Auscultating breath sounds at the midclavicular, midaxillary lines and bases, comparing one side to another

    Abdomen

    • Palpating each quadrant of the abdomen, comparing one side to the other
    • Looking for guarding, rigidity, tenderness, distention or masses

    Pelvis

    • Compressing & depressing the pelvis looking for instability or crepitus
    • Checking for priapism, bleeding or incontinence

    Lower extremeties

    • Palpating the femurs circumferentially
    • Visualizing the lower legs for any obvious injuries

    Upper extremeties

    • Palpating each humerus circumferentially
    • Visualizing the lower arms for any obvious injuries

    Back/Log Roll

    • On my partner's count we're going to log roll the patient so I can assess the posterior and we can package for transport
    • Palpating the back of the skull, assessing the spine for any stepoff
    • Checking for any incontinence or any other bleeding
    • Positioning a board underneath the patient and again on my partner's count we'll roll the patient onto the board

    Transport

    • We're going to transport this patient
    • En-route we'll get a baseline set of vitals and reasses every 5 minutes
    • We'll do a detailed physical exam which includes a more thorough head-to-toe assessment
    • Reassess any interventions & manage additional injuries
